# Phase 0 Research Kit

## Objective

Validate whether gaming friend groups and their hosts experience the proposed problems strongly enough to move, and whether the managed-versus-sealed privacy model is understandable.

The target sample is 12 members, 8 hosts/moderators, and at least 5 prototype usability sessions. Do not recruit children; participants must confirm they are at least 18 for research even though the eventual service targets 16+.

## Consent script

> We are researching communication tools used by gaming friend groups. Participation is voluntary. Do not share private server content, account credentials, personal identifiers, or details about minors. Notes will be synthesized without your handle. You may skip any question or stop at any time. May I take anonymized notes for product research?

Record only `yes` or `no`; do not record identity documents or exact birth dates.

## Member interview, 30 minutes

1. Tell me about the last time your group moved from the game into voice chat.
2. What made joining easy or frustrating?
3. Which Discord surfaces do you use every week? Which do you avoid?
4. Show how you catch up after being away. Where do you lose context?
5. When do you mute a server or channel? What would make you return?
6. Describe a Discord change that disrupted you. What specifically became harder?
7. What would have to work on day one before your group would try an alternative?
8. What data, history, roles, or integrations would you refuse to leave behind?
9. What do “managed,” “sealed,” and “end-to-end encrypted” mean to you?
10. Which feature would you pay a community host to keep available, if any?

## Host/moderator interview, 40 minutes

1. Walk through creating and maintaining the current community.
2. Which permission and onboarding tasks create mistakes?
3. Which bots are mandatory, and what first-party gap does each fill?
4. Walk through the most recent moderation incident without sharing identifying content.
5. What evidence, audit, notification, and appeal information was missing?
6. How would you safely invite the group to a new service?
7. Which structure could be imported automatically? Which content should never be copied?
8. How should ownership survive an absent, compromised, or banned owner?
9. What community costs feel legitimate? What would feel like a hostage fee?
10. What would make you stop a migration during the first week?

## Survey

Use a five-point scale from strongly disagree to strongly agree, followed by one forced ranking.

- Joining friends in voice is reliably fast.
- Discord's current layout helps me find what needs attention.
- I understand why I receive each notification.
- I trust Discord not to make disruptive interface changes.
- Community permissions are understandable.
- First-party moderation tools are sufficient.
- I can export what I need before leaving a service.
- I understand which conversations the service provider can read.
- My group would try an alternative if structure and roles could be imported.
- My group would temporarily use a clearly labeled bridge while moving.

Rank: voice reliability, performance, privacy, moderation, migration, interface stability, notifications, customization, mobile support, bot compatibility.

## Prototype tasks

1. Accept an invitation and explain the displayed privacy/retention information.
2. Find `campfire`, send a message, and locate its notification history.
3. Join `Ready Room`, select a microphone, and explain the encryption indicator.
4. Determine why a hypothetical member cannot post in a channel.
5. Report a message and find where an appeal would appear.
6. Switch to a sealed room and explain which features are unavailable.

Measure task completion, time, wrong turns, confidence, and the participant's own explanation. Do not lead with product terminology before asking them to interpret it.

## Synthesis rubric

For each observation record:

- Segment: member, host, or moderator.
- Frequency: isolated, repeated, or dominant.
- Severity: inconvenience, repeated friction, migration blocker, or safety risk.
- Existing workaround and its cost.
- Proposed plan decision affected.
- Confidence and contradictory evidence.

A roadmap decision changes only when evidence is repeated across at least three participants or a single observation exposes a critical safety/accessibility failure.
